# HR Management Processes

Manager-facing HR process guidance — how to run a performance improvement process. For employee-facing policy (leave, benefits, termination entitlements), see [[shared.hr-policies-and-benefits]]; every employee is entitled to that information, this document is about how managers execute the process, not what employees are owed.

## Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) process

When an employee's performance is unsatisfactory, follow this sequence — do not skip steps or escalate faster than the timeline allows without HR involvement:

1. **Verbal warning.** Manager documents the conversation and the specific performance gaps in writing (for the manager's own record). Set clear expectations and a 14-day timeline.
2. **Written warning.** If no improvement after the verbal warning. Formal written notice specifying: performance issues, expected improvements, support offered, a 30-day timeline, and the consequences of non-improvement. The employee signs an acknowledgment. Written warnings are legally important for termination defense — do not skip this step even if a case feels obvious.
3. **PIP.** If no improvement after the written warning. Formal 30–60 day improvement plan with specific measurable goals, weekly check-ins, manager support and resources, and clear pass/fail criteria.
4. **Termination.** If PIP goals are not met. Follow the Thai labor law termination requirements in [[shared.hr-policies-and-benefits]] — notice period, severance by tenure, and the no-severance exceptions.

All HR decisions at every step (warnings, PIP, termination) must be documented in writing — this protects both the employee and the company if the decision is later challenged.
